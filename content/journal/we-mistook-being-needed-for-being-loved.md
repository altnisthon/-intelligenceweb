I used to think being indispensable was a beautiful thing.

The friend everyone could call, the colleague who always stayed late, the partner who understood without needing to be told.

It felt noble; it also felt exhausting.

No one tells you that there is a quiet danger in becoming everyone's safe place while never learning where yours is.

Somewhere along the way, many of us learned that love had to be earned.

By being useful. By being agreeable. By anticipating everyone else's needs before they became requests.

Perhaps that is why saying "no" feels heavier than carrying the weight of everyone else's expectations.

Research tells us that chronic people pleasing is often rooted in our need for belonging and fear of rejection, which means it was never really about kindness.

Sometimes it was survival dressed beautifully enough to receive compliments.

I no longer want relationships where I am only valued for what I can carry. I want relationships where I am allowed to put things down.

Maybe love was never meant to feel like proving your worth. Maybe it was always meant to feel like finally resting in it.
