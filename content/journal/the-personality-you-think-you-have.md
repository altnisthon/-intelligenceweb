For the longest time, I thought personality was something fixed. I believed people were simply confident or shy. Calm or anxious. Independent or needy.

Then I started meeting enough people to notice something.

Many of us were introducing our coping mechanisms as our identity.

The woman who says she never needs anyone.

The man who jokes before every vulnerable conversation.

The colleague who cannot rest without feeling guilty.

Perhaps these are not personalities.

Perhaps they are love letters written to old wounds.

Children are astonishingly creative.

If love feels unpredictable, they learn to become perfect.

If emotions feel unsafe, they become invisible.

If approval becomes survival, they become high achievers.

Years later, the child disappears.

The strategy remains.

This is why understanding our innate tendencies matters. Not because they define us forever. Because they help us separate who we are from who we had to become.

I find that strangely comforting.

It means healing is not about becoming someone else. It is about meeting the person who has been waiting underneath survival all along.
