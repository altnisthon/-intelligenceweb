When I was younger, I believed there were only two kinds of people.

The good ones and the bad ones.

Life, in all its inconvenient honesty, has spent years unteaching me that belief.

I have met kind people capable of cruelty.

I have met gentle people who became ruthless when cornered.

I have met those who have lied out of fear and those who have told painful truths out of love.

Somewhere along the way, I stopped asking whether people were good or evil. I became more interested in what they were feeding.

Perhaps that is the uncomfortable truth about being human.

There is a monster in all of us.

Not the kind that hides beneath the bed.

The kind that lives quietly beneath our virtues.

It knows how to become angry. It knows how to seek revenge. It knows how to manipulate, to control, to dominate, to wound.

Most of us would rather pretend it isn't there. But pretending has never made anything disappear.

Carl Jung once wrote that we do not become enlightened by imagining figures of light, but by making the darkness conscious. The parts of ourselves we refuse to acknowledge do not vanish. They simply learn to speak in ways we no longer recognize.

Perhaps emotional maturity is not the absence of darkness. Perhaps it is the courage to know its name.

We often celebrate softness. Kindness. Patience. Forgiveness. And we should.

But kindness without boundaries becomes self abandonment.

Patience without discernment becomes permission.

Forgiveness without wisdom sometimes becomes another invitation to be hurt.

The opposite is equally dangerous.

Strength without compassion becomes tyranny.

Confidence without humility becomes arrogance.

Protection without restraint becomes violence.

Good and evil have never lived on opposite ends of a spectrum.

They have always shared the same home.

Us.

Every day, they sit at the same table.

Every day, they ask to be fed.

The question is not whether you have a monster.

The question is what kind of monster you are raising.

Will it bare its teeth at anyone who disagrees with you?

Will it lash out because it has never learned another language for pain?

Or will it stand quietly between someone vulnerable and the world that wishes to harm them?

There is a reason we admire people who possess great strength but choose gentleness.

Not because they are incapable of destruction.

Because they are.

And they decide, over and over again, to wield their strength with wisdom instead.

A sword locked away forever is not peace.

It is simply an unused sword.

Peace is someone who knows exactly what they are capable of and chooses restraint until protection becomes necessary.

I think we misunderstand goodness.

Goodness is not innocence. Goodness is not never feeling anger. Goodness is not always being agreeable. Goodness is having every reason to become cruel and deciding that your pain will not become someone else's inheritance.

Perhaps that is why emotional intelligence matters so deeply.

It is not about becoming endlessly positive. It is about recognizing the monster before it begins making decisions on your behalf. It is noticing when fear disguises itself as certainty.

When pride disguises itself as confidence.

When woundedness disguises itself as justice.

The world does not need more people pretending they have no darkness.

It needs more people who have looked directly at it and learned how to hold it with responsibility.

Maybe that is what courage has always been.

Not killing the monster.

Teaching it who it serves.

Because there will be moments when the people you love need someone fierce enough to stand between them and harm.

There will also be moments when the greatest act of courage is refusing to let your own wounds decide how the story ends.

We are not angels learning how to become human.

We are humans learning what to do with everything we carry.

The light. The shadow. The tenderness. The rage.

Perhaps becoming a better person has never meant destroying one to preserve the other.

Perhaps it has always meant choosing, day after day, which one gets fed. Perhaps that's enough for today.

Not to become less human.

But to become someone whose strength is guided by compassion, whose anger is disciplined by wisdom, and whose monster remembers that it was never meant to rule.

Only to protect.
